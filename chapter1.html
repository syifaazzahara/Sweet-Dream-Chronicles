Lost in a Lush Garden

Chapter 1

When I opened my eyes, the world was already watching me.

Light filtered gently through layers of impossibly green leaves, spilling gold across my skin. The air smelled sweet—too sweet—like flowers blooming all at once, their fragrance thick enough to taste. Somewhere nearby, water trickled softly, repeating the same rhythm over and over, as if it had forgotten how to stop.

I didn’t move at first.

Something felt… arranged.

Like this moment had been waiting for me.

“Finally.”

The voice came softly, almost relieved.

I turned.

There he was—upright, striped, bright orange against the overwhelming green. Smiling too widely, standing just a little too still.

Tigger

“I was wondering how long you’d sleep this time,” he said, tilting his head. His tone was light, but his eyes didn’t blink.

“This… time?” I echoed.

I pushed myself up slowly, my body responding as if it belonged to me, even if nothing else did.

“Where am I?” I asked.

He took a small step closer.

“In a garden,” he said.

I frowned.

“I don’t remember anything.”

“I know.”

The answer came too quickly.

Too naturally.

I looked at him carefully now.

“You know?” I asked. “How?”

For a split second, something flickered across his face—not confusion, not surprise.

Recognition.

Then it was gone, replaced by his usual grin.

“You always say that at the beginning,” he replied.

My chest tightened.

“At the beginning of what?”

He didn’t answer immediately. Instead, he circled me slowly—not threatening, not aggressive—just… attentive. Like he was making sure every detail of me was exactly where it should be.

“You’ll remember,” he said eventually. “You always do.”

Something in the way he said always made the garden feel quieter.

I looked around.

The lush greenery stretched endlessly, flowers swaying even when there was no wind. Paths curved in unnatural ways, disappearing behind walls of vines that seemed just a little too deliberate.

Everything was beautiful.

Perfectly beautiful.

Uncomfortably so.

“Have we met before?” I asked.

Tigger stopped moving.

For a moment, he didn’t speak.

Then he smiled again—brighter this time, almost exaggerated.

“Of course we have,” he said. “We spend a lot of time together.”

My stomach twisted.

“I don’t remember you.”

“That’s okay,” he replied quickly. “You will.”

He leaned in slightly.

“I remember enough for both of us.”



His soft orange paws were trying to reach me but stopped midway, afraid that if he gets even a fraction closer, ill break, like a fragile ceramic doll.
