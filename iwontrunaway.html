No, I wont run away this time.

The Everchanging garden

The urge was there—quiet, persistent, like a reflex I couldn’t fully remember learning. The garden stretched endlessly around me, beautiful in a way that felt deliberate, like every leaf had been placed with care. The air was warm, fragrant, almost comforting.

And still—



something beneath it all felt… rehearsed.

I stood still.

And behind me, I could feel him.

Watching.

Waiting.

“…You’re not moving,” said Tigger, his voice softer than I’d ever heard it before.

“No,” I replied.

A pause.

“That’s new.”

“I think so too.”

I turned slowly to face him.

He wasn’t smiling as wide this time. No exaggerated grin, no playful bounce. Just standing there, tail barely swaying, eyes fixed on me—but not in that intense, overwhelming way from before.

Something had changed.

Or maybe—

something had finally stopped.

“I don’t want to run,” I said.

“You don’t have to,” he answered quickly.

“I don’t want to remember either.”

That made him hesitate.

Just for a second.

“…Okay,” he said, more carefully now.

I took a breath.

“I don’t know what the right thing to do is,” I continued. “And every time I try to figure it out… it feels like I’m just repeating something.”

He didn’t interrupt.

Didn’t correct me.

So I kept going.

“So this time… I’m not choosing,” I said. “You decide.”

Silence.

Not heavy.

Not tense.

Just… quiet.

Tigger blinked.

Once.

Twice.

Like the words hadn’t quite settled yet.

“…Me?” he asked.

“Yes.”

“You want me to decide what happens next?”

“Yes.”

Another pause.

Longer this time.

His tail, which had always been in motion—flicking, swaying, twitching—went completely still.

“No running?” he asked softly.

“No.”

“No trying to leave?”

“No.”

“No… forcing yourself to remember?”

I shook my head.

“No.”

Something in him shifted.

Not like before—not cracking, not breaking—

but loosening.

Like tension he had been holding for far too long finally slipped free.

“Oh,” he said quietly.

Just that.

Oh.

He looked down at the ground, as if unsure what to do with his own thoughts.

“You’ve never done that before,” he added.

“I know.”

“And you’re really… letting me choose?”

“Yes.”

I held his gaze.

“For real.”

The garden felt different again.

But not in the way it had during the chase.

Not bending.

Not closing in.

Just… still.

Like it, too, was waiting.

Tigger took a small step forward.

Then stopped.

Then another.

Slow.

Careful.

Not the energetic bouncing, not the exaggerated movements—

just simple, uncertain steps.

“I thought…” he started, then stopped himself.

“You thought what?” I asked.

He looked up at me again, and for the first time—

there was no performance in his expression.

“I thought you’d keep trying to leave,” he said.

“Maybe I did before.”

“…Yeah,” he whispered.

A quiet settled between us.

Then, slowly—

he sat down.

Right there, on the soft grass.

Not dramatically.

Not playfully.

Just… sat.

Like he didn’t need to perform anymore.

“That makes it harder,” he admitted.

“How?” I asked.

He glanced up at me, something almost shy in his eyes now.

“Because I don’t have to stop you,” he said. “I just have to… choose.”

“And you don’t know what to choose?”

He shook his head slightly.

“I know what I want to choose,” he said.

“What is it?”

Another pause.

This one felt fragile.

“I want you to stay,” he said quietly.

The words weren’t sharp anymore.

Not desperate.

Not controlling.

Just honest.

“I figured,” I said.

“…But I don’t want to force you,” he added quickly.

That caught me off guard.

“You didn’t seem too worried about that before,” I said.

“I know.”

No denial.

No excuse.

“I just… didn’t know what else to do.”

His voice softened even more.

“I thought if I made everything perfect, you wouldn’t want to leave.”

I looked around.

The lush garden.

The endless beauty.

The careful design.

“It is perfect,” I said.

He flinched slightly.

“Yeah,” he murmured. “That’s the problem, isn’t it?”

I stepped closer.

Then, slowly—

I sat down in front of him.

For a moment, he froze.

Like he didn’t expect that.

Like he wasn’t sure if it was allowed.

“You said you get to choose,” I reminded him.

“…Yeah.”

“Then choose.”

He looked at me—

really looked this time, not studying, not analyzing—

just seeing.

And something in his expression softened even further.

The tension in his shoulders disappeared.

His tail curled loosely around him instead of flicking.

“…Can I try something?” he asked.

“What?”

He hesitated.

Then, very slowly—

he leaned closer.

Not invading.

Not overwhelming.

Just… careful.

Like approaching something fragile.

“You can say no,” he added quickly.

I didn’t move away.

That was enough.

He exhaled softly—

and then, almost hesitantly—

rested his head against my shoulder.

Lightly.

Barely any weight.

Like he expected me to disappear if he leaned too hard.

The contact was warm.

Unexpectedly gentle.

“I think…” he whispered, voice quieter than ever, “I don’t want to rebuild anything right now.”

I stayed still.

Listening.

“I don’t want to chase you,” he continued. “I don’t want to stop you. I don’t want to make you forget.”

A pause.

His voice dropped to almost nothing.

“I just want this… for a little while.”

The garden didn’t shift.

Didn’t react.

For once—

it wasn’t controlling the moment.

He was.

And strangely—

he wasn’t controlling it at all.

His grip, if it could even be called that, was soft.

Almost like a kitten curling up somewhere safe for the first time.

Not possessive.

Not afraid of losing—

just… resting.

“You’re not choosing for me,” I said quietly.

“…No,” he admitted.

“You’re choosing for yourself.”

“…Yeah.”

Another pause.

Then, softer—

“And hoping you stay anyway.”

I didn’t answer.

Not because I didn’t want to—

but because, for once—

there was no pressure to.

The garden remained lush.

Beautiful.

Unchanging.

And beside me—

Tigger didn’t watch.

Didn’t wait.

Didn’t chase.

He just stayed.

Quiet.

Warm.

Careful.

Like something that had finally learned—

it didn’t need to hold on so tightly

to keep something from disappearing.
